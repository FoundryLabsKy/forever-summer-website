Where we started

Two pages, and the best story untold.

The audit of foreversummer.com and the shop's public footprint found:

  • A near-empty site

    Only a home page and an "FS Girls" page were live. No shop tour, no story, no way to act.

  • The drop was invisible

    New styles arrive weekly in strictly limited quantities — the shop's defining mechanic lived only on Instagram, never on its own site.

  • The wrong address story

    The site said "45 Market St, George Town" — technically true, but shoppers search for Camana Bay, and the landmark ("next to Starbucks") appeared nowhere.

  • Mixed service signals

    Reviews included a glowing staff shout-out and separate complaints about fitting-room service. The site took no position.

  • Identity drift

    Hours conflicting between sources (10–6 vs 10–7); the Facebook page named "ForeverSummer.com" instead of the brand.

Every finding is cited in AUDIT-forever-summer.md.

The changes, and why

"Here Friday. Gone Sunday."

  • The drop, productized

    A "New this week" page explains the weekly limited-drop model, bridges to Instagram where drops go live, and offers a first-look email list. Why: it's the shop's real buying model (sourced) — and no local competitor puts a drop cadence on the web. Real scarcity, honestly told, beats fake countdown timers.

  • A friendly fitting-room promise

    "Try anything. Try it twice. Ask for another size out loud." Plus a hold-request form that's honest about short, first-come holds. Why: reviews flagged service inconsistency; the site now sets the standard publicly — and celebrates the staff shout-out review right beside it.

  • Found where people look

    "45 Market St, The Paseo, Camana Bay — next to Starbucks," repeated consistently, with structured data and a maps link. Why: "George Town" framing hid the shop from Camana Bay searches; one wayfinding line ends the three competing landmark descriptions found in the audit.

  • FS Girls, kept and grown

    The teen line keeps its own page — "first-job budgets welcome" — in the same drop rhythm. Why: teens/young adults are a stated target audience; it's also the argument for the owner's to-do of opening TikTok.

  • Affordability said out loud

    Copy leans into "fashion you can actually afford" — the shop's own positioning — rather than hiding price. Why: in an import-duty economy, attainable pricing is a real differentiator, and it's the brand's own tagline.

The design system

Built from Catherine's own words.

The owner opened the shop to capture "that nostalgic feeling of long summer days and nights filled with fun and freedom." The visual system is that sentence, drawn:

Sun-bleached cream — field Golden-hour amber — secondary Dusk blue — deep anchor Hibiscus — CTAs only
  • A retro sunset signature

    Striped sun arcs — part 70s postcard, part beach towel — carry the nostalgia brief in the logo mark, hero and favicon.

  • Warm, rounded, quick

    Fraunces (soft editorial serif) over Karla, pill buttons and 10–18px corners: playful and youthful — deliberately the opposite of big-sister Sand Angels' sharp gallery look, so the family reads as a family, not clones.

  • Motion with manners

    The drop marquee is the one moving element; it pauses entirely for visitors who prefer reduced motion.

Proof & next steps

Measured, not vibes.

  • Accessibility

    Every color pair passes WCAG AA (verified programmatically); axe reports zero critical or serious issues on all six pages; keyboard-operable, 44px+ touch targets.

  • Performance

    One ~55 KB HTML file with zero image requests — the striped sun is inline SVG. Instant on a phone outside Starbucks.

  • Honesty

    Drop photo slots are labeled placeholders for the shop's own weekly photos; the single review quote is verbatim from Google, flagged for owner approval; conflicting hours are flagged, not guessed.

  • Owner to-dos

    Confirm hours · supply logo & drop photos · rename the Facebook page to "Forever Summer" · claim Google Business Profile · consider TikTok for FS Girls.
